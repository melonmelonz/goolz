Problem Brainstorm

Three problems I've seen or lived. Real pain, real people, real workarounds.

Problem 1

People coming out of incarceration can't navigate modern technology. The digital world moved on while they were inside. Smartphones, two-factor auth, app stores, cloud accounts, digital IDs. Stuff everyone else absorbed gradually over years hits them all at once on day one.

Who: Returning citizens in the first 1-6 months after release. Roughly 600,000 people per year in the US alone. Disproportionately people who went in before smartphones were everywhere.

How often: Every single day. Every interaction with the modern world requires a device they barely know how to use. Applying for jobs, signing up for benefits, even getting on a bus in some cities.

Current workaround: Asking whoever is nearby. A family member, a case worker, a guy at the library. Their PO might hand them a printed sheet of URLs. Sometimes a reentry program runs a one-hour "computer class" that covers almost nothing. Mostly they just avoid technology until they can't.

Problem 2

People learning kernel/systems programming can't find a clear path through the docs. The knowledge exists but it's scattered across mailing lists, LWN articles, kernel.org, in-tree Documentation/ files, random blog posts from 2009, and commit messages. None of it is organized by "what do I need to know to do X."

Who: Early-career systems programmers, CS students trying to contribute to the kernel, self-taught devs moving down the stack. Maybe a few thousand people at any given time actively trying to break in.

How often: Every time they sit down to learn. Which is daily when you're in the thick of it. You spend more time searching for the right doc than reading it.

Current workaround: Asking on IRC/Matrix and hoping someone answers. Grepping the source tree. Reading LWN archives. Following breadcrumbs from one commit message to the next. Trial and error with a QEMU VM until something clicks.

Problem 3

Small community orgs that run donation drives or supply closets can't track inventory against requests. Churches, mutual aid groups, community fridges. They collect stuff, people need stuff, but matching supply to demand is chaos.

Who: Volunteer coordinators at churches, mutual aid networks, community centers. The person who "just handles it." Usually one or two people per org, maybe a dozen orgs in a mid-size city.

How often: Weekly during collections, monthly during distributions. Spikes around holidays and disasters.

Current workaround: A shared Google Doc that three people have edit access to and nobody updates consistently. Paper sign-up sheets. Group texts. Keeping it all in one person's head and hoping they don't burn out and quit.

▶ After sharing Problem 1 got the strongest reaction from my partner. They said they'd seen it firsthand with a family member. Problem 2 is real but niche. Problem 3 is real but there are already tools for it (Airtable, etc.) even if people don't use them. Moving forward with Problem 1.
