Verbal Mini-Demo -- The Codex CLI Session I Ran

Two minutes in the breakout. I picked the localStorage task from Ex 7. The goal sentence I wrote down before opening the agent: "In src/TodoApp.jsx, persist todos to localStorage so it survives a page refresh; load on mount, save on every change." Single file, single feature. The kind of scope you can hold in your head.

The prompt named the file, pointed at the existing useState pattern, and asked for a diff before applying. What came back was mostly right. A lazy useState initializer pulling from localStorage.getItem("todos") with a try/catch fallback, plus a useEffect writing on change. But on the way through, it had also reformatted an unrelated arrow function in addTodo. Not a bug. Just noise. I rejected that piece, re-prompted "only the localStorage changes, leave everything else alone." Second diff was clean. Applied.

My partner asked what I would do differently next session. Two things came up. The fence -- "do not modify any other functions, even formatting" -- belongs in the original prompt, not the review. And I should have asked for a one-line comment on the try/catch, because corrupted localStorage data is the case it is catching and in two weeks I am not going to remember that. Small stuff. The kind of thing that compounds across sessions if you let it.

The Thing That Clicked Today

The .filter().filter().map() chain in Ex 4. That was the moment React stopped feeling like a UI thing and started feeling like data plumbing. The visible list is a projection of the underlying array through a couple of predicates. Filter buttons don't change the list. They change what slice gets rendered. The data underneath never moves. That is a separation I want in every system I touch. One place holds the truth, the view is a function of it, the events are the only thing that get to mutate it. Yesterday I sort of believed that. Today I felt it on the screen.

The Codex CLI piece that landed: scope does most of the work. The bad-session example in the lecture wasn't bad because the agent is dumb. It was bad because "make this app better" forces the agent to make up the goal, and now I am reviewing its goals against my intentions instead of a diff against a spec. The cure isn't a smarter prompt. It is a smaller one.
